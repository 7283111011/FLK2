Core Topics in Land Law

Nature of Land and Estates

English land law distinguishes between real property (land and interests in land) and personal property. Legal estates are limited to two kinds: freehold estates in fee simple absolute in possession and leasehold estates (terms of years absolute). A valid transfer of a legal estate must be made by deed complying with section 52 of the Law of Property Act 1925, while equitable interests may arise under trusts or contracts. Conveyances of land must also comply with registration requirements under the Land Registration Act 2002 or, for unregistered land, with the doctrines of deeds and the doctrine of notice.

Mark as read
True or False: Only freehold and leasehold estates can exist as legal estates in land and transfers of legal title must be made by deed.

Title to Land and Registration

The Land Registration Act 2002 introduced a system of title registration whereby ownership and many interests in land must be recorded on the register. Freehold estates and leaseholds of more than seven years are registrable. Overriding interests, such as legal easements and some leases, bind a purchaser of registered land even though they do not appear on the register; the 2002 Act aims to reduce such interests and lists those that override first registration and subsequent dispositions separately. Unregistered land continues to be governed by title deeds, land charges and the doctrine of notice; equitable interests must be protected by registration as land charges to bind purchasers.

Mark as read
True or False: Overriding interests may bind a registered title even if they are not entered on the register, though the Land Registration Act 2002 restricts the categories of such interests.

Co‑ownership and Trusts of Land

Where more than one person owns land, they do so as joint tenants or tenants in common. A joint tenancy is characterised by the four unities (possession, interest, title and time); each co‑owner is entitled to the whole and the right of survivorship operates so that on death their share automatically passes to the others. By statute, legal co‑ownership must be a joint tenancy, but the beneficial interest may be held as a joint tenancy or as a tenancy in common in shares. A tenancy in common gives each co‑owner a distinct share without the right of survivorship. Joint tenants may sever the tenancy to become tenants in common, for example by serving a notice of severance under section 36(2) of the Law of Property Act 1925. Disputes between co‑owners as to sale or occupation may be determined under sections 14–15 of the Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996.

Mark as read
True or False: In a joint tenancy each co‑owner is treated as entitled to the whole land and the right of survivorship applies, whereas in a tenancy in common each owns a distinct share without survivorship.

Proprietary Rights: Easements, Covenants and Mortgages

Certain non‑possessory rights in land are termed proprietary rights. Easements are rights enjoyed by one landowner over another’s land; to be valid, an easement requires a dominant and servient tenement, the right must accommodate the dominant land, the owners must be different and the right must be capable of forming the subject matter of a grant. Freehold covenants are promises relating to the use of land; the benefit and burden of positive and restrictive covenants may pass to successors in title under common law and equity, subject to rules such as the requirement of intention to benefit and the doctrine in Tulk v Moxhay. Mortgages are security interests over land: the lender takes a legal charge and has powers to sell or possess the property upon default, but owes duties to act in good faith and to obtain a proper price. The priority of mortgages is determined by order of registration; mortgagors have an equitable right to redeem.

Mark as read
True or False: An easement requires a dominant and servient tenement and must accommodate the dominant land, and the priority of mortgages is determined by the order in which they are registered.

Leases, Licences and Remedies

A lease (tenancy) grants exclusive possession of land for a term at a rent, creating a legal estate. In contrast, a licence merely permits occupation without conferring proprietary rights. The relationship between landlord and tenant is underpinned by privity of contract and privity of estate; covenants in the lease bind successors depending on whether they touch and concern the land. Remedies for breach of covenants include forfeiture (by peaceable re‑entry or court order), action for rent arrears, specific performance and damages, but the landlord must comply with procedural requirements such as serving a section 146 notice. Leases may be terminated by effluxion of time, surrender (express or implied) or merger when the same person acquires the reversion and the leasehold. Break clauses in leases must be exercised strictly in accordance with their terms.

Mark as read
True or False: A lease confers exclusive possession for a term and may be forfeited for breach of covenant, whereas a licence merely grants permission to occupy without a proprietary estate.

Business Tenancies and Security of Tenure

Business tenants occupying premises for the purposes of a business enjoy statutory security of tenure under Part II of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954. Section 24 provides that a business tenancy will not end automatically at expiry but will continue until terminated in accordance with the Act; section 25 enables the landlord to serve notice to terminate or offer a new tenancy, and section 26 allows the tenant to request a new tenancy. The court may grant a new tenancy for a term not exceeding 15 years on terms it considers just if the parties cannot agree. The landlord may oppose renewal on specific grounds such as own occupation or redevelopment, and compensation may be payable if certain grounds are proved.

Mark as read
True or False: Under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 business tenants are entitled to request a new tenancy, and if terms cannot be agreed the court may grant a new tenancy for a term of up to 15 years.
